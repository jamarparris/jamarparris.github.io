Getting Cash Abroad? How to Save Hundreds in Foreign Currency Fees Each Year

Apr 12th, 2015

Getting foreign currency from banks or a bureau de change is generally quite easy. In my experience and others, however, they extract a hefty premium for their services. I know quite a few people who just shrug and accept that as a cost of traveling abroad. In addition, if you exchange too much money during the trip, then it either ends up at home in a jar or you go back and exchange it again at another awful rate so they make money on both the outward and return leg.

It’s actually not that difficult to do much better than this. One of the things I discovered a few years ago is that it is generally much cheaper to just go to a local ATM once you land in a foreign country and withdraw the funds you need with your debit card. This is because the rate bank ATMs use is a bulk rate determined by the network issuing your card.

That being said, many ATMs will charge you a foreign fee for this transaction but you can avoid that too. :)

Online banks have become very popular over the years, offering higher interest rates, low or no fees and in my experience, better service. Because many of these online banks do not have their own branches, they typically allow you to use any ATM at no fee and they generally even reimburse you for any fee that the ATM charges you in the U.S.

Some financial institutions actually go further than this and reimburse you for any fees charged worldwide. Fidelity and Schwab are great examples of this. These accounts are so good that I now use them as my primary accounts even within the U.S. Whether I go 7 feet from home or 7000 miles, every ATM is a free ATM.

Now, whenever I land in a new country, I just stop at the ATM on the way out of the airport and only take out as much as I’ll need for a day or two. If I run out mid-trip, I just find another ATM.

Check them out, if you’re a frequent international traveler, this could literally save you hundreds of dollars per year and hopefully they’ll be less random leftover bills in your currency jar, unless you like to keep them as momentos.
